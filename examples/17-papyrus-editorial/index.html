Manifesto

In an age of infinite scroll, we believe in the power of the printed page.

There is a particular stillness that happens when a reader turns the page of a carefully bound book. It is not the stillness of absence; it is the stillness of attention. We founded Papyrus in 2009, in a small room above a printer's workshop in Verona, because we believed then — and still believe now — that certain sentences deserve the dignity of paper and ink.

Our catalogue is deliberately small. We publish seventeen titles a year, and not one more. Every manuscript is read by at least three editors, often aloud. We reject more than nine hundred submissions for every one we accept. This is not arrogance; it is a form of respect — for the writer, for the reader, and for the strange, patient magic of a book in the hand.

We print on uncoated paper from responsible forests. We sew our bindings. We believe that a book is, first and foremost, an object. And that an object made with care asks for, and rewards, care in return.

Featured Author / Spring 2025

Elena Vásquez

Elena Vásquez writes about small towns, long afternoons, and the quiet industries of the night. Her essays have appeared in every serious literary review on both sides of the Atlantic, and her fiction has been translated into eleven languages. The Quiet Hours is her fourth book and her first with Papyrus.

She lives between Oaxaca and Edinburgh with two dogs, one typewriter, and a growing collection of nineteenth-century guidebooks to nowhere in particular.

A Note from the Publisher

When Elena's manuscript arrived, late one autumn, it arrived as a sealed envelope — no covering letter, no return address, only a note typed on a blue ribbon: "These are the hours I have kept." We have kept them with her, and now we offer them to you.

The essays in this collection move slowly. They ask for patience. In the second piece, "On the Hours that Have No Name," Vásquez describes an afternoon of waiting in a small Spanish village that is, I think, one of the finest things written in any language this decade.

— Alessandro Moretti, Editor-in-Chief. Milan, February 2025.
